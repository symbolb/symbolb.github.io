How does a project's delay change its rate of return (ROR)?

When evaluating an economic opportunity using net cashflows from financial forecasts, the Net Present Value method is commonly used by engineers to value a project. The famous formula for discounting projects is:

$$NPV=\sum_{k=0}^n\frac{C_k}{(1+i)^k}\tag{1}$$

where `NPV` is the Net Present Value, `C` is the net cashflow, `k` is the period in years or months forecasted, `n` is the maximum period of the economic evaluation, and `i` is the discount interest rate.

Three useful results from Eq.1 include:

  1. Net Present Value `NPV`
  2. Rate of Return `ROR`
  3. Payback Period

The `ROR` is determined by setting `NPV` to zero and solving for `i` iteratively.

If a project is delayed a year, and all the net cashflows remain unchanged, the `NPV` will decrease by a factor of `1/(1+i)` as shown.

$$NPV_{delayed}=\frac{1}{(1+i)}NPV\tag{2}$$

So what happens to the `ROR` when all the cashflows, including the initial capital investments, are shifted in the schedule by a year? Because the `NPV` is set to zero, it can be algebraically proven that the `ROR`, unintuitively, doesn't change.

$$NPV_{delayed}=\frac{1}{(1+i)}\sum_{k=0}^n\frac{C_k}{(1+i)^k}=0\tag{3}$$

$$NPV_{delayed}=\sum_{k=0}^n\frac{C_k}{(1+i)^k}=NPV\tag{4}$$

So, again assuming that the net cashflows remain unchanged, an unstarted project delay only impacts the `NPV` and not the `ROR`. `ROR` is usually the target result teams try to optimize but need to be careful not to overvalue it compared to `NPV`.
